With trademarks, they are judge on whether they are "inherently distinctive" or if they are "descriptive".  So something that is descriptive would be using a location like "Melbourne Dental Services" or descriptive terms such as  "Comfortable Boots" as your business names.  These would not be able to get trademarks. (You could still call your business those names) The way to think about it is if another business would want to use that word to describe their product you can not block them for doing so.  So inherently distinct is the other end of the spectrum in that it has nothing to do with the product.  Think "Apple Computers", before this company started apples had nothing to do with computers.  or Nike for shoes and sporting apparel.  Before the company the work Nike had nothing to do with those products.  The other test for trademarks is that it is not deceptively similar or substantially identical. The FSF/OSS points were also interesting as my group is looking at Sentencing Software in A2. In the US, they use proprietary software that takes a range of variables and then spits out a 'likelihood' of the offender reoffending which is then taken into account when the judge sentences them. Neither the judge, defendant or prosecutor knows how the software comes up with this likelihood figure and, despite an appeal from a man who was jailed for six years, the algorithm remains secret. Given the severe consequences that result from the use of this algorithm, this looks like a perfect example of where they could use an open source framework. That way, the information would 'be free' and all those involved would be aware of how the likelihood figure was achieved.

                        An interesting part of patent law (an an important ethical issue) is the rent seeking that can result.  For example, a pharmaceutical company will have a spend about $1 b to get a drug to market and could take over a decade for this to occur.  This means that the 20 year patent has to be taken out well before the drug ever gets to market.  Upon getting to market they may have only 5-10 years.  So what can happen is they hold back potential improvements as a way to extend the patent.  As an example Omeprazole (Losec) and Esomeprazole (Nexium) are made by the same company (AstraZenica) and are both off patent.  However, they are virtually identical.  Omeprazole contains both left and right handed molecules while Esomeprazole contains only left handed ones.  Only the left handed one works, and your body converts the right handed version anyway.  AstraZenica purposely released a slightly inferior product, so they could get a new patent on the slightly better version.  Is it ethically wrong for AstraZenica to do this?

            Hi Shyam, The exam will be mostly practical i.e. you will have to write plenty of code by hand.                 Hi Mikhail, Thank you, I appreciate the information. However, I would like to raise some thoughts. I believe modern teaching methods should aim to make students understand fundamental concepts and skills. Unfortunately, the reality at RMIT is that teachers still mostly just expect students to memorise stuff for the exam, knowing exactly that the memorised knowledge will mostly be lost two weeks after the exam anyway. With that in mind, could you please explain to all of us, how can students develop IT and industry skills by memorising class methods and properties of a 20-year old, outdated GUI technology that they are almost certainly never going to use in the age of mobile apps and HTML 5? What benefit does that provide to students or the IT industry overall? Could you please explain why in a course called “Software Architecture” students have to learn the methods and properties of socket classes in a specific language instead of actually learning the fundamentals of client-server and distributed architecture, API Standards, message exchange patterns, stateless vs. stateful design, etc.? In what way is memorising specific class methods and properties related to software architecture and design at all? So, when developing course content and exam material, please consider these points. In addition to that, I would seriously suggest considering renaming this course to “Java Programming 2”, because let’s be honest, it has absolutely nothing to do with architecture. Regards,Pascal

                                        Hi All,

                                        Few points related to your final exam preparation:

                                        • We do not release past exam papers in SADI; however, a sample exam paper covered during our last week's chat session can be found here: Collaborate Timetable and Summaries
                                          • There are two versions: before.pdf containing the original questions, and after.pdf which includes answers from the chat session.
                                          • Your actual/real exam paper will follow a very similar structure and topic coverage as the one covered during the chat.
                                        •  A rough outline of the exam structure:
                                          • Duration - 2 hrs (plus 10 mins reading time).
                                          • Closed book.
                                          • Total marks: 50
                                          • Structure:
                                            • Part A: 10 multiple choice questions - 1 mark each;
                                            • Part B: Small programming/practical exercises related to the material covered in the lectures – approx. 23 marks in total;
                                            • Part C: A large programming exercise i.e. a complete GUI-based application developed utilising the MVC and Observer patterns – approx. 17 marks.

                                        The best way to prepare for the exam is to revisit all the lecture material (slides and video recordings), as well as your own assignment implementation. You should also make every effort to watch the final lecture in which Caspar covers our exam topics and examples.

                                        Good luck on the exam!

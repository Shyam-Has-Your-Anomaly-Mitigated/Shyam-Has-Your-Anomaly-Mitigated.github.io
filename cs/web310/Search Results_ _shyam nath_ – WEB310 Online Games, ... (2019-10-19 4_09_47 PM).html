    FUNDAMENTAL COMPONENTS OF THE GAMEPLAY EXPERIENCE: ANALYSING IMMERSION                                                                                      
    The word gameplay symbolises that games, and play, are indeed the same concept.
    SCI model of gameplay experience: sensory, challenge-based, and imaginative immersion.
    Word; gameplay, experience, immersion, ontology.
    Klimmts' three-level model of the enjoyment of playing digital games: the first level of which consists of the interactive input-output loops, the second of cyclic feelings of suspense and relief, and the third is related to the fascination of a temporary escape into another world.
    Pines', and Gillmores', types of experiences: participation (active versus passive), and connection (absorption versus immersion).
    Four realms of experience: entertainment (absorption, and passive participation), educational (absorption, and active participation), aesthetic (immersion, and passive participation), and escapist (immersion, and active participation).
    Presence assess the immersivity of a system; presence is a psychological experience of non-mediation (the sense of being in a world generated by the computer instead of just using a computer).
    Immersion is the sensation of being surrounded by a completely other reality that takes over all of our attention, our whole perceptual apparatus.
    Presence = Immersion.
    Presence is used in the context of teleoperations, and relies on the metaphor of transportation.
    Immersion is used in the context of digital games, because it more clearly connotes the mental processes involved in gameplay.
    McMahans' 3 conditions for immersion in digital games: the conventions of the game matching the user expectations, meaningful things to do for the player, and a consistent game world.
    According to Douglas, and Hargadon; genre fiction encourages players to form hypotheses, and expectations, and pleasures of immersion derive from the absorption within a familiar schema.
    According to Salen, and Zimmerman, meaningful play occurs when the relationships between actions, and outcomes, are both discernable (indicates letting the player know what happens when they take action), and integrated (tying those actions and outcomes into the larger context of the game).
    According to Järvinen, Heliö, and Mäyrä, the audiovisual, functional, and structural playability, can be seen as prerequisites for gameplay immersion, and rewarding gameplay experience.
    Browns', and Cairns', classification that categorises immersion into gameplay in three levels of involvement; ranging from engagement, via engrossment to total immersion, where total immersion = presence.
    The flow-like experiences related to gameplay could be called micro-flow, or gameflow.
    Funk, Pasold, and Baumgardner, created a gameplay experience questionnaire in order to investigate the effects of exposure to fantasy violence; they developed a measure that concentrates on psychological absorption, but it does not differentiate between different kinds of gameplay experiences even though the theoretical model presented suggests that there are at least two kinds of experiences: absorption and flow.

    THE GAME, THE PLAYER, THE WORLD: LOOKING FOR A HEART OF GAMENESS.

    The classic game model was never valid; gamification means there are no limitations for what is a game, and if you can describe it using game theory then it must also be a game.

    Lots of people play games on their telephones, and I've played games on airplanes, and we even played tetris on our television using the remote control; all before The Sims, so Jesper Juul is short sighted in their attempt to define games.

    This is a video of a "computer" game where they use an artificial intelligence that is not make believe, nor separate, to productively remove uncertainty: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sw7UAZNgGg8
    If freedom defines games, then there are no games; "play this game, or else", and all of a sudden it isn't a game? that is not separate!

    To challenge all three distinct components of goals; valorization of the possible outcomes can be neutral (draw, tie,..), player effort may be automated (the second player in tic tac toe, when the first player takes the middle), and attachment is broken when the player wins too easily, or feels cheated when they lose (en passant in chess is not widely known, but such an insignificant move of an insignificant piece that they probably wouldn't've won anyway).

    The new definition of 6 game features.
    1) Rules: this is agreeable.
    2) Variable, quantifiable outcome: infinite is both variable, and quantifiable (infinite lists from lazy functions in Haskell).
    3) Value assigned to possible outcomes: this should be merged into "variable, quantifiable outcome", which is already redundant in that all outcomes are variable, and quantifiable.
    4) Player effort: player effort depends on the skill level of the player, and how many times they have played the game (boredom).
    5) Player attached to outcome: neuroscience is largely unknown, therefore player attachment is dependent on the players' psychology, which cannot be known, nor fixed, in advanced.
    6) Negotiable consequence: this is redundant in that it has no influence over whether, or not, a set of rules is a game.

    I agree most with the writers mentioned in the conclusion who "have claimed that games are forever indefinable or ungraspable".
    Even toys can be gamified; since playing length is measurable, and there may be other statistics, and accomplishments.
    Pokémon GO is a fictive world in the real world, and has real world consequences; the players' position in the real world has changed, and where they continue their life outside of the game influences their life in the real world.
    Geo caching is a real world version of Pokémon GO without any fictive aspects.
    Johnny English Strikes Again features a virtual reality simulation that has real world implications, and satisfies most, if not all, descriptions of games; kill all the baddies to win, where the real world interactions were not included in the design of the simulation.
    The rules of games are not for any of the reasons described; rules serve the collaboration of multiple players, where the game itself is considered a player; which supports games like Progress Quest.

    Semi-official cheat codes are not the only means at a players' disposal to modify the rules of the game.
    Games can be accidentally, or intentionally, turing complete: https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=16383072
    Where this is accidental; the discovery of turing completeness involves the exploitation of security vulnerabilities in the game itself.
    Metaprogramming is probably most common in Emacs, where users use Elisp, the language of Emacs, to modify Emacs itself; this is a fundamental characteristic of all Lisp dialects in general, and is where Emacs gets it from, but user modifications to Lisps are less likely than user modifications to Emacs.

    Rules are used to make rules, but rules themselves can't exist without rules. The other characteristics of play are either disproven by counterexample, or are themselves rules; which makes rules the superset, or even the universal set, of characteristics of play.

    1. Free; isn't a characteristic of play, because reverse psychology can make a player feel free even though their freedom is socially engineered. Psychological manipulation is an important factor when considering freedom; a free choice of a card can be forced by the magician, the organisation of products on shelves at the supermarket can influence consumer purchasing decisions, and adwareness can also influence consumer purchasing decisions.
    2. Separate; isn't a characteristic of play, because the limits of space, and time, can be infinite, and this effectively makes it negligible. Limitations are just rules. In fact, playing life does not support any limitations of space, or time, that are fixed in advanced; this is supported by Heisenbergs' uncertainty principle.
    3. Uncertain; isn't a characteristic of play, because a skilled chess master will always defeat someone who has never even heard of the game, let alone played it before. #counterexample
    4. Unproductive; isn't a characteristic of play, because team-building cooperative play can improve group dynamics. #counterexample
    5. Governed by rules; the one true characteristic of play. Logic is the rationality for the interconnectedness of all things.
    6. Make-believe; isn't a characteristic of play, because real world role-playing, like playing doctor during a medical emergency, is not make-believe (even if the player isn't even a real doctor, and is just applying bandages from a first-aid kit). #counterexample

    There is no difference between play, and other forms of human activity; all human activity involves playing roles.     TO BE, OR NOT TO BE; THAT IS THE QUERY…
    Due to the informalities of natural language; the English word "play" means more than what it means in a gaming context, and even within gaming contexts it is inconsistent.
    There is a fine line between a play, and a game; non-interactive stories (text books, picture books, flip book animations, plays, videos), versus interactive story games (choose your own text adventures, visual novels, interactive movies), versus games without stories (toys, unending open world games like Minecraft).
    Then there is playing a game, versus playing a musical instrument, versus playing a role (in a play, versus in a game).
    Play is non-serious in many contexts, but there are exceptions; like playing audio (musical instruments, recorded music, radios), playing video (television, film, BD), and even playing games (as opposed to play=fun, where playing games that require grinding is not fun).
    A film playing at a movie theatre, verus playing a film at a movie theatre; such is the nature of natural language.
    Play can be a form of entertainment, but also a form of education (as in play fighting amongst animals); but in both contexts play is pretending, whereas playing soldier is not necessarily pretending (children imitating adults, versus adults playing a societal role model to support social engineering propaganda).

    THE DEFINITION OF PLAY
    The law of averages on the fifth page is a fallacy, and this brings into question the validity, strength, soundness, and cogency, of the author; therefore I must assume they are fawlty.
    Roger Caillois defines play as an activity which is essentially free, separate, uncertain, unproductive, governed by rules, and make-believe.
    Tic Tac Toe is a game where the first player controls the second player after first playing the middle tile, and the second player taking their first move; therefore only the second move is uncertain to the first player, and while the first players' first move is certain to the second player, every other move is uncertain until the first players' second last move. It is also possible to predict, and anticipate, players; artificial intelligence has been used to profile poker players. However, a better argument against uncertainty is competitive play, where experience is a significant factor, as are advantages; a player may have a height advantage, for example.
    Educational games such as play fighting is certainly not unproductive, nor are memory-based card games which improve memory; there are many more experiential benefits of play that serve pesonal development, where all players may gain.
    An adult playing soldier is not make-believe.
